What is Lunar New Year?


Lunar New Year, also known as Chinese New Year or the Spring Festival, is one of the most important traditional festival in many East Asian countries, including China, Vietnam, Korea, etc. The date of Lunar New Year is determined by the lunar calendar, so it varies each year, however typically falls between January 21 and February 20.


The celebration marks the beginning of the lunar new year and the end of winter, symbolizing a time of renewal and fresh starts. Each year is associated with one of the 12 Chinese zodiac animals, which cycle in a 12-year sequence. These animals are Rat, Ox, Tiger, Rabbit, Dragon, Snake, Horse, Goat, Monkey, Rooster, Dog, and Pig.


During Lunar New Year, people engage in various customs and traditions, such as cleaning their homes to sweep away bad luck, decorating with red and gold colors (symbolizing good fortune and wealth), and enjoying festive meals with family and friends. Fireworks, dragon and lion dances, and the giving and receiving of red envelopes (containing money) are also common traditions.


Lunar New Year is a time for family reunions and is often considered the most important holiday in East Asian cultures. The length and specific customs of the celebration can vary between countries and regions, but the underlying theme of welcoming the new year with joy, positivity, and the hope for good fortune remains consistent.
