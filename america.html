Eating traditional food during Tet (Lunar New Year) is deeply rooted in cultural and symbolic significance. The foods prepared and consumed during Tet carry meanings and symbols that are believed to bring good luck, prosperity, and blessings for the coming year.


Tet is a time when families come together to celebrate, and the preparation and sharing of traditional foods are communal activities. The act of making these dishes often involves multiple generations working together, creating a shared experience that strengthens family bonds and traditions. These culinary rituals contribute to a sense of continuity and stability, providing a link between the past, present, and future.


The festive atmosphere during Tet is emphasized by the consumption of these traditional foods. The variety of flavors, textures, and colors in these dishes creates a joyous celebration. It is a time when the entire community is immersed in their culinary heritage, creating an atmosphere of shared joy and positivity.


So, by sharing these dishes with family and friends, people spread happiness and good vibes for the new year. Tet becomes a time of joy, with everyone celebrating their cultural heritage through the delicious and meaningful foods they share.

Thịt Kho Hột Vịt


Thịt Kho Hột Vịt, also known as braised pork with eggs is a popular dish in many Asian cuisines, and it is enjoyed throughout the year, not just during the Lunar New Year. This dish typically involves simmering pork and eggs in a flavorful braising liquid until the meat is tender and the eggs absorb the delicious flavors.


ingredents:

  • 1.5 to 2 pounds of pork belly or pork shoulder, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 6 to 8 hard-boiled eggs, peeled
  • 4 cloves of garlic, minced
  • 1 thumb-sized piece of ginger, sliced
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1/4 cup dark soy sauce (for color)
  • 1/4 cup rice wine or Shaoxing wine
  • 2 tablespoons oyster sauce
  • 2 tablespoons sugar
  • 2 cups water or chicken broth
  • Green onions for garnish (optional)
  • Sesame oil (optional)

instructions:

  1. Prepare Ingredients: Cut the pork into bite-sized pieces, peel the hard-boiled eggs, and mince the garlic.
  2. Sear the Pork: Heat a bit of oil in a large pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown the pork pieces on all sides until they develop a golden color.
  3. Add Aromatics: Add the minced garlic and sliced ginger to the pot. Stir and cook for a couple of minutes until fragrant.
  4. Braise the Pork: Pour in the soy sauce, dark soy sauce, rice wine, oyster sauce, and sugar. Mix well. Add water or chicken broth to cover the pork. Bring the mixture to a boil.
  5. Simmer: Once boiling, reduce the heat to low and cover the pot. Allow the pork to simmer for about 1.5 to 2 hours or until the meat is tender. Stir occasionally and add more water if needed.
  6. Add Eggs: Gently add the peeled hard-boiled eggs to the pot during the last 30-45 minutes of cooking. Allow the eggs to absorb the flavors of the braising liquid.
  7. Finish: Once the pork is tender and the eggs are infused with flavor, check the seasoning and adjust if necessary. If desired, drizzle a bit of sesame oil for extra flavor.
  8. Serve: Serve the braised pork with eggs over steamed rice. Garnish with chopped green onions or chili if you like.

Bánh Tét


Bánh Tét is a traditional Vietnamese rice cake, this festive dish is made from glutinous rice and filled with various ingredients, creating a cylindrical shape. Bánh tét is then wrapped in banana leaves and typically steamed until it becomes a dense, sticky rice cake.


ingredents:

  • 2 pounds pork belly, sliced into thin strips
  • 1 pound mung beans, soaked and peeled
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Fish sauce and sugar for seasoning
  • 4 cups glutinous rice (also called sweet rice or sticky rice)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • Coconut milk (optional)
  • Banana leaves, cleaned and cut into large rectangle
  • Kitchen twine or banana leaf strips to secure the cake

instructions:

  1. Prepare the Filling
    • Season the pork belly slices with salt, pepper, fish sauce, and sugar. Let it marinate for at least 30 minutes.
    • In a pan, cook the marinated pork until it's no longer pink. Set aside.
    • Cook the mung beans in water until soft. Mash them into a paste, seasoning with salt and sugar.
  2. Prepare the Rice
    • Rinse the glutinous rice until the water runs clear.
    • Mix the rice with salt and, if desired, coconut milk for flavor.
  3. Assemble
    • Lay out a banana leaf and place a layer of rice on it.
    • Add a layer of mung bean paste, followed by a layer of cooked pork belly.
    • Repeat the layers until you reach the desired size, making sure to leave some space at the top.
    • Fold the sides of the banana leaf over the filling and secure with kitchen twine or banana leaf strips.
  4. Steam
    • Place the wrapped bánh tét in a steamer and steam for about 6-8 hours until the rice becomes sticky and the filling is cooked through. Make sure to add water to the steamer as needed.
  5. Cool and Slice
    • Allow the bánh tét to cool before slicing. The slices can be served on their own or pan-fried for a crispy texture.

Bánh Chưng


Bánh Chưng is another traditional Vietnamese rice cake, very similar to bánh tét, it is a square-shaped cake made from glutinous rice, mung beans, and pork, all wrapped in banana leaves and then steamed or boiled. Bánh chưng is more popular in the North, while bánh tét is more popular in the South.


ingredents:

  • 2 pounds pork belly, cut into thin, square-shaped slices
  • 1 pound mung beans, soaked and peeled
  • Salt, pepper, fish sauce, and sugar for seasoning
  • 4 cups glutinous rice (sweet rice or sticky rice)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • Water for soaking
  • Banana leaves, cleaned and cut into square pieces
  • Kitchen twine or bamboo strips to tie the cakes

instructions:

  1. Prepare the Filling
    • Season the pork belly slices with salt, pepper, fish sauce, and sugar. Let it marinate for at least 30 minutes.
    • In a pan, cook the marinated pork until it's no longer pink. Set aside.
    • Cook the mung beans in water until soft. Mash them into a paste, seasoning with salt and sugar.
  2. Prepare the Rice
    • Rinse the glutinous rice until the water runs clear.
    • Mix the rice with salt and let it soak in water for at least 4 hours or overnight.
  3. Assemble
    • On a clean work surface, lay out a piece of banana leaf and place a layer of soaked glutinous rice.
    • Add a layer of mung bean paste, followed by a layer of cooked pork slices.
    • Cover with another layer of rice.
    • Fold the banana leaf over the layers, creating a square-shaped package.
    • Secure the package by tying it tightly with kitchen twine or bamboo strips.
  4. Steam
    • Place the wrapped bánh chưng in a large pot of water and boil for about 6-8 hours, ensuring the water level stays above the cakes. Alternatively, you can steam the cakes for a similar amount of time.
  5. Cool and Slice
    • Allow the bánh chưng to cool completely before slicing it into pieces. The slices can be served as is or pan-fried for a crispy outer layer.

Gà Luộc


Eating boiled chicken, or "gà luộc," during Tet (Lunar New Year) is a traditional practice in many Vietnamese households. Boiled chicken is a popular and easy-to-prepare dish.


ingredents:

  • Chicken (choose fresh chicken, about 1.5 - 2 kg)
  • Salt (about 1-2 tablespoons)

instructions:

  1. Prepare the chicken: Rinse the chicken thoroughly with cold water to remove any impurities. You can pluck the chicken feathers by boiling water and dipping the chicken into hot water for a few minutes.
  2. Boiling water: Bring a large pot of water to a boil. Add salt to the boiling water (about 1-2 tablespoons), ensuring the water is evenly salted.
  3. Boil the chicken: Once the water is boiling, place the chicken into the boiling water. Reduce the heat to medium and simmer for about 40-60 minutes, depending on the size of the chicken. To check if the chicken is cooked, you can use a toothpick to pierce the thigh and check if the juices run clear.
  4. STurn off the heat and soak the chicken: When the chicken is cooked, turn off the heat and leave the chicken in the hot water for about 10-15 minutes to allow the meat to absorb the flavors.
  5. Rinse with cold water: After soaking, place the chicken in cold water or an ice bath to cool the meat and enhance its flavor.
  6. Season to taste: Boiled chicken can be served with sweet and sour fish sauce, salt and pepper lime, or any sauce of your preference.
